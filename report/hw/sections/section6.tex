\section{Sampling-Time Effect and Tustin Redesign}

The discrete-time controller of Section~5 used the simplest approximations: the
error derivatives were computed with backward differences and the adaptation law
was integrated with forward Euler.

\subsection{Performance Degradation with Increasing Sampling Time}

Figure~\ref{fig:s6_degradation} shows the tracking error \(e(t)\) and the
filtered error \(z(t)\) for \(T_s\) from \(0.01\) to \(0.05~\mathrm{s}\). As seen below, the peak
tracking error grows as \(T_s\) increases.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figs/hw/section6_degradation.png}
\caption{Tracking error \(e(t)\) (top) and
filtered error \(z(t)\) (bottom) for increasing sampling periods.}
\label{fig:s6_degradation}
\end{figure}

\noindent The main cause is the second backward difference used to estimate
\(\ddot{e}\):
\begin{equation}
\ddot{e}[k] \approx \frac{e[k]-2e[k-1]+e[k-2]}{T_s^{2}}
\label{eq:s6_secdiff}
\end{equation}
Since the gain grows like \(1/T_s^{2}\). As
\(T_s\) grows the loop loses phase margin, and at \(T_s\approx0.06~\mathrm{s}\)
the closed loop becomes less stable. The forward-Euler integration of the adaptation law adds a smaller error of the same kind.

\subsection{Improved Design - Tustin Discretizations}

To recover good performance without reducing \(T_s\), the controller is
re-discretized using Tustin transform:
\begin{equation}
s \;\longleftarrow\; \frac{2}{T_s}\,\frac{z-1}{z+1}
\label{eq:s6_tustin}
\end{equation}
which gives a more accurate approximation of the continuous-time dynamics than
the forward-difference rule \(s\leftarrow(z-1)/T_s\).
The backward-difference approximation used in the baseline controller becomes
sensitive to noise and numerical errors as the sampling time increases. To
improve robustness, the error derivatives are obtained using a first-order
filtered derivative instead. Applying the Tustin transformation to the filter gives:
\begin{equation}
\dot{\hat{e}}[k]=
\frac{a\big(e[k]-e[k-1]\big)-(1-\tau a),\dot{\hat{e}}[k-1]}{\tau a+1},
\qquad
a=\frac{2}{T_s}
\label{eq:s6_dirty}
\end{equation}
and the second derivative is obtained by applying the same filter to
\(\dot{\hat{e}}[k]\). This approach provides smoother derivative estimates and
reduces the sensitivity of the controller to large sampling times.
The adaptation law is also discretized using the Tustin method instead of the Forward Euler approximation. The resulting update equation is:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\theta}[k]=\hat{\theta}[k-1]
-\frac{T_s}{2},\Gamma\big(\varphi[k]z[k]+\varphi[k-1]z[k-1]\big)
\label{eq:s6_trap}
\end{equation}
which uses information from both the current and previous samples. Compared to
Forward Euler integration, this provides a more accurate approximation of the
continuous-time adaptation law and improves the behavior of the controller for
larger sampling periods. The remaining control calculations are performed as in Section~5.

\subsection{Simulation Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:s6_fix} compares the baseline and redesigned controllers for
$T_s=0.05~\mathrm{s}$. The figure shows both the output
tracking and the corresponding tracking error.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figs/hw/section6_tustin_fix.png}
\caption{Output tracking
(top) and tracking error (bottom) for the baseline and Tustin-based
controllers.}
\label{fig:s6_fix}
\end{figure}

\noindent The redesigned controller exhibits improved transient performance and
a smaller peak tracking error than the baseline implementation. The response is
smoother, with reduced overshoot and faster convergence to the reference model.
The tracking error also decays more rapidly, indicating that the controller
better preserves the behavior of the original continuous-time design.
At very small sampling periods the baseline controller can achieve slightly
lower tracking errors, since the derivative filter introduces a small phase
lag. However, the purpose of the redesign is to improve performance when the
sampling period becomes large, and in this operating range the redesigned
controller consistently provides better tracking accuracy. Overall, the Tustin-based implementation successfully
reduces the performance degradation caused by the discrete-time realization and
allows the controller to operate with larger sampling periods.
